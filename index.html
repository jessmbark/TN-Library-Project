Reading Between the Lines

How are kids interacting with Tennessee public libraries?

By: Jessica M. Barker

When I was a kid, I loved my local library. It turns out that the National Institute of Museum and Library Services surveys thousands of public libraries across the U.S. every year and they provide a treasure trove of data with those survey results.

I wanted to see how kids in Tennessee interact with the library today, if they do at all. I used the most recent data available, which is from fiscal year 2021-2022.

Not only are children well-represented as a percentage of borrowers, but they're well-represented in terms of circulation as well.

I was also curious about children's programs in the library, however, the data actually did not include any information on the number of children's programs or their attendance after 2021 for most programs. I reached out to my own local librarian for insight, but she could only provide me more complete data for my local branch.

I was able to get some information about Tennessee's Summer Reading Program, though. The Summer Reading Program is not just meant for children, but it does "encourage reading during the pivotal summer months," according to the state Summer Reading Program's website.

Given that language, I think insight about the typical library's Summer Reading Program provides insight about how children interact with libraries. Overall, libraries hosted more than 4,500 Summer Reading Program events in 2022.

  • The typical library hosted 7 Summer Reading Program events
  • They used an average budget of $900
  • That comes out to just under $130 per event

Libraries are able to use promotional materials from the Collaborative Summer Library Program (CSLR) to get help advertising their Summer Reading Programs.

Despite the prevalence of children as borrowers and libraries' dedication to crafting Summer Reading Program events with tight budgets, I found that not every library in the state has dedicated staff to help children or even young adults.

That disparity inspired me to look to the national data to see where Tennessee ranks in terms of total librarians per library.

As more recent data from the IMLS survey is released, it would be interesting to follow up and see if Tennnesee has since hired more librarians, particularly children's librarians.
